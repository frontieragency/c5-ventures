Why this actually matters

On a phone, a phone number can be a button.

Tap to call, no dialing

On a phone, a business's number can be set up as an actual button — tap it, and the call just starts. Someone standing in front of a leaking window doesn't have to remember a number, switch apps, or type anything. That doesn't happen automatically; it has to be built that way on purpose.

A quote form that actually works with a thumb

A short form built for a small screen means someone can ask for a quote right then, standing in their kitchen — not "later, at a computer" they may never get back to. Tiny text boxes and dropdowns that are hard to tap turn a two-minute form into a five-minute fight, and people give up.

Why Google cares which version you see

Google looks at the phone version to decide what shows up.

Google has said this directly: it looks at the mobile version of a site — not the desktop one — to decide what shows up in search results. Google finished rolling this out across every site on the internet in October 2023. In practice: if a phone number or a way to ask for a quote is missing, buried, or broken specifically on the phone version, that's the version Google is judging — no matter how good the site looks on a computer.

What a slow phone experience costs

A slow site on a phone loses people fast.

People leave fast

Google's own data: as a page's load time on a phone goes from 1 second to 10 seconds, the chance someone leaves before it finishes loading goes up 123%. More than half of mobile visits get abandoned once a page takes longer than 3 seconds.

Fixing it moves real numbers

Google has published real examples of businesses that fixed a slow mobile site: one saw mobile signups roughly double, another saw a real jump in mobile orders, a third cut how often people left immediately nearly in half. These aren't hypothetical — they're what changed once the mobile experience actually got fixed.

Why this happens

Most people build the desktop version and call it done.

It's natural — the site gets built and reviewed on a laptop, because that's what the business owner and whoever built it are both looking at during the process. Nobody opens it on their own phone afterward, mostly because there's no reason to; the site already looks "done." That's exactly how a phone number ends up buried, or a quote form ends up too small to tap correctly, for years, without anyone noticing.

Common pitfalls

The same few mistakes, over and over.

Never opening your own site on your own phone

Resizing a browser window on a laptop isn't the same as loading the real site over a real phone connection.

A phone number that isn't actually tappable

If the number on the page isn't set up as a tap-to-call button, someone has to copy it, switch apps, and dial it manually — and a lot of people just won't.

A quote form that's painful on a small screen

Tiny fields and dropdowns that are hard to tap turn a quick form into a frustrating one, and people give up halfway through.

Assuming Google judges the site you see on your computer

It doesn't. Google looks at the phone version specifically — that's the one that has to actually work.
