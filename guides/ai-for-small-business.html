Guide

AI is good at some of this. Not all of it.

Every part of running a business isn't equally suited to it. Here's where it genuinely removes friction for a small trade business, and where handing it to a person is still the right call.

Adoption is real, trust is the holdup

Small businesses are behind — and cautious for a real reason.

Small businesses make up 44% of U.S. GDP but have lagged larger companies in AI adoption, per Anthropic's own reporting on its small-business offering. In the same research, roughly half of small business owners surveyed named data security as their single biggest hesitation about using AI at all — not cost, not complexity. That caution is worth taking seriously, not talking someone out of.

Where it's a legitimate fit

Repeatable, well-defined, high-volume tasks.

Answering the phone and the first message

Routine calls, texts, and booking requests follow predictable patterns — the same handful of questions, over and over. That's exactly the kind of repetition worth handing off.

Following up on a lead

A lead that goes cold because nobody had time to call back isn't a judgment problem — it's a follow-through problem, and follow-through is where automation earns its keep.

Paperwork and admin

Invoice chasing, drafting routine correspondence, organizing records for tax season — Anthropic's own published workflow list for small businesses centers almost entirely on exactly this kind of task.

"It's freeing up things that used to be a lot of very tedious clerical work for more value-add tasks."

— Ryan Olson, Technology and Innovation Manager, MidCentral Energy

Watching the numbers

Monitoring cash position, reconciling accounts, flagging when a marketing campaign's performance shifts — spotting patterns in data a busy owner doesn't have time to check every day.

"Not only could it problem-solve for me, it also showed me problems I didn't know I had."

— Brian Ludviksen, COO, Purity Coffee

Where it's not the right tool

Judgment calls a person actually has to make.

Diagnosing what's actually wrong

Whether a repair is structural, what caused it, what it'll actually take to fix — that needs someone who's physically looked at the job, not a generated guess standing in for an inspection.

Anything with real liability attached

Final pricing on a safety-critical job, a legal or insurance-facing decision — the kind of call that needs someone accountable for it, by name.

A customer who's upset

A script can handle a routine question. It can't de-escalate a genuinely frustrated customer the way someone who actually cares about the outcome can.

The work itself

Google's own quality guidelines reward real, first-hand experience over content that just describes it — the same principle applies off the page. Nothing replaces someone who's actually done the job.

Common pitfalls

The same few mistakes, over and over.

Picking it up because it's trendy

The businesses that get real value started with an actual problem — a phone that goes unanswered, invoices that pile up — not a vague feeling that they "should be using AI."

Using it for the calls it's worst at

The businesses that get burned are usually the ones that let it make a call — a diagnosis, a final price — that needed a real person's name attached to it.

Never telling the customer

If someone figures out on their own that they were talking to a script the whole time, that lands worse than the script just doing its job well would have. Being upfront about it costs nothing.

Assuming it needs zero oversight

Handing something off isn't the same as walking away from it. The businesses that get the most out of it still check in on what it's actually doing.

The point of all this

Outcomes, not a tech stack.

The goal was never "use more AI." It's picking the handful of places it genuinely removes friction — the phone that never gets answered, the lead that never gets a follow-up — and leaving the rest to the people who've actually done the work.
