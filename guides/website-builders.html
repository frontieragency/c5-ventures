Guide

Wix, Squarespace, GoDaddy, GoHighLevel — not the same tool.

Each one gets picked for a real reason — it's cheap, it's easy, an agency already set it up. The problem is what happens after: whether any of them actually get you found, and what it costs when they don't.

What actually happens

People pick these for good reasons. Here's what happens next.

Wix

It's drag-and-drop — about as easy as building a slide in PowerPoint or Google Slides. It's cheap, and the templates already look professional. That's why people pick it. Here's what actually happens: whether Google can even see the site is one switch, hidden in the settings — off by default in a lot of setups. The business goes months without a single call from Google, and the site looks totally fine, so nobody thinks to check. This isn't rare — in 2015, real Wix sites disappeared from Google search results the same way, and Google had to respond publicly.

"We're working on resolving things here on our side... we'll be recrawling these sites a bit faster to get the dropped pages back."

— John Mueller, Google, responding to the 2015 Wix disappearance

Squarespace

The designs are genuinely the nicest-looking of the bunch. If you care how the site looks, that's a real reason to pick it. Sonshine Glass & Mirror's old site was built on Squarespace. It looked clean and professional — nothing about it looked broken. But here's what actually happened, straight from their own Google data: over 14 months, that site got 111 total impressions on Google, and exactly one click. A nice-looking site and a site that actually gets found are two different things. After a full rebuild, that same business's daily visibility on Google went up roughly 500x — in under two months. See the full case study.

GoDaddy Website Builder

It's the easiest starting point — a salesperson walks you through it, or it's already bundled with the domain you bought. Here's what actually happens: Google itself found that in 2020, only about 4 out of 10 GoDaddy sites loaded fast enough to pass Google's own bar for a decent site. That means the site itself was working against the business, before anyone even read a word on it. GoDaddy fixed a lot of this by 2021 — but there's no way for a business owner to know which kind of site they ended up with.

GoHighLevel

Usually a marketing agency sets this up, because it's genuinely good at one thing: capturing a lead and texting them back automatically. Here's what actually happens: months later, the business still doesn't show up when someone searches their exact service in their exact city. In practice, that usually means no way to see how the site is doing on Google at all, no way to tell which page actually brought in a lead, and the site itself never showing up in a Google search in the first place. That's not a bug — it's by design.

"We do not offer split testing on websites."

— HighLevel Support Portal, on its own Websites tool

Why this happens

Picking one of these wasn't the mistake.

Most business owners aren't trying to become a website expert. They want to check the box that says "we have a website" and get back to running the business — which is completely reasonable. That's exactly why these problems sit there for years: the site gets built, it looks done, and nobody ever opens it again to check whether it's actually working.

Common pitfalls

The same few mistakes, over and over.

The visibility switch nobody flips

The site is finished and looks great. But if Google can't see it, none of that matters — and there's no way to tell just by looking at it.

Paying for a plan that can't fix problems

The cheap plan looks just as nice as the expensive one — until something breaks. Then the cheap plan can't fix it.

A slow site loses people before they read a word

If the site takes too long to load, people leave before they ever see what you actually offer.

A lead tool standing in for a website

It's great at texting a lead back. It was never built to show up when someone searches Google in the first place.

What to actually expect

Six months in. Twelve months in.

None of this happens overnight — not on these platforms, and not with us either. A site built to actually get found usually takes a few months before real traffic shows up, and it keeps growing well past that. If anyone promises results in the first few weeks, that's not a real promise. What actually matters is whether the site was built toward that outcome at all — because some of these platforms were never going to get there, no matter how much time passed.
