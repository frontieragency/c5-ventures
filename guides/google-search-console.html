Guide

What Google Search Console actually is.

Not a setting inside your website — its own free, separate dashboard from Google. Log in, and it tells you the one thing nothing else can: exactly how Google sees your site, what's actually showing up in search, and what's broken. Almost no business owner has ever opened it.

Set up once

Usually a one-time job for whoever manages your site.

These three don't need to be done by you personally — if someone else manages your website, this is a fair thing to just ask them to confirm.

1

Prove to Google the site is actually yours

Before Search Console shows you anything, there's a one-time step to prove ownership. The most reliable way is a small entry added to the domain's settings (technically called a DNS TXT record) — it survives a site redesign or a switch to a new platform, where quicker methods can get wiped out the next time anyone touches the site.

2

Hand Google the full list of your pages

A sitemap is just a list of every page on the site, in one file. Submitting it (under "Sitemaps") means Google gets that list directly, instead of having to stumble onto pages on its own by following links around — pages that are hard to stumble onto that way can stay invisible for a long time.

3

Check for a red flag Google's already raised

Under "Security & Manual Actions" — if anything shows up here, it overrides everything else on this list. A manual penalty or a hacked-site warning means nothing else gets fixed properly until this is cleared first.

Check monthly

The screens that actually tell you something.

4

See what Google is actually showing

The Pages report lists every page Google found, and — separately — every page it chose not to include in search results, with a reason why. If a page isn't on the "included" list, it cannot show up in a Google search. Full stop, no matter how good it is.

5

See what people actually searched to find you

The Performance report shows the real words and phrases people typed into Google before landing on the site, how often that happened, and roughly where the site showed up in the results. It's the closest thing to reading a customer's mind before they call.

6

Check the phone experience specifically

The Mobile Usability report flags text too small to read, buttons too close together to tap accurately, content that doesn't fit the screen — real problems on the device more than half of the site's visitors are using.

7

Check the site-speed score

Google's own measurement of how fast a page actually feels to load and use. A slow site doesn't just show up lower in search — it loses the visitor before the quote form even finishes loading.

Common mistakes

What quietly costs visibility.

Never checking it after setup

Most accounts were verified once, years ago, and never opened again — the errors and opportunities inside go unread indefinitely.

Ignoring the pages Google looked at but skipped

Google visits a page, then decides not to include it in search results — usually a sign of a real content problem, not a fluke. That page won't show up in search until it's fixed.

Not telling Google about real updates

After a meaningful change to a page, there's a button (the URL Inspection tool) that asks Google to take another look sooner — instead of waiting for it to notice on its own schedule, which can take a while.

Seeing interest without checking the ranking

A search term with a lot of impressions but almost no clicks usually means the site is showing up on page 2 of the results, not page 1 — worth knowing before assuming the page itself isn't good enough.
