Guide

The Google Search Console checklist.

Search Console is free, and it's the only place that tells you the truth about how Google actually sees your site. Most business owners have never opened it. Here's what to check, and in what order.

Set up once

Get verified and submitted before anything else matters.

1

Verify your property

A DNS TXT record is the most reliable method and survives a site redesign or platform switch — an HTML file or meta tag can get wiped out the next time someone touches the site.

2

Submit your sitemap

Under Sitemaps, submit sitemap.xml directly. This is what tells Google every page you actually want indexed, instead of relying on it to stumble onto them by following links.

3

Check for manual actions and security issues first

Under Security & Manual Actions — if either shows anything, it overrides everything else on this list. A manual penalty or a hacked-site flag means the rest of your SEO doesn't matter until it's cleared.

Check monthly

The reports that actually tell you something.

4

Pages report — what's actually indexed

Shows every page Google has and hasn't indexed, and why not (duplicate content, crawled but not indexed, blocked by robots.txt). A page that doesn't show up here can't rank for anything, no matter how well it's written.

5

Performance report — queries, clicks, position

Shows the actual search terms people used to find you, how often you showed up, and where you ranked. This is the single best source of what to write more of — real queries you're already getting found for, just not high enough.

6

Mobile usability

Flags text too small to read, clickable elements too close together, content wider than the screen — real problems on the device more than half of your visitors are using.

7

Core Web Vitals

Measures how fast the page actually feels to load and use. A slow site doesn't just rank worse — it loses the visitor before the quote form even loads.

Common mistakes

What quietly costs visibility.

Never checking it after setup

Most accounts were verified once, years ago, and never opened again — the errors and opportunities inside go unread indefinitely.

Ignoring "crawled, not indexed"

A page Google visited but chose not to index is usually a real content or duplication problem, not a fluke — and it means that page will never show up in search until it's fixed.

Not requesting indexing after real changes

After a meaningful update to a page, use the URL Inspection tool to request re-indexing instead of waiting for Google to notice on its own schedule.

Reading clicks without position

A query with high impressions and low clicks usually means you're ranking on page 2 — worth knowing before assuming the content itself isn't working.
