Guide

Google, Angi, Thumbtack — not the same reach, not the same cost.

They get talked about like interchangeable places to "get found online." Real, sourced numbers say otherwise — on reach, on cost, and on what a customer actually experiences on each.

The reach

Google says it handles more than 5 trillion searches a year. Angi's own investor reports show about 111,000 active pros and roughly 16 million projects a year across its platform. Thumbtack's own numbers: about 300,000 pros and more than 15 million completed five-star jobs. Different companies measure different things, but the gap in scale between "how people use Google" and "how people use a directory site" is real.

The cost

A Google Business Profile costs nothing to create or maintain — no fee to appear, no fee to show up in a search. Angi and Thumbtack both charge per lead, and neither publishes official pricing — it's negotiated by trade, by market, by lead. That's real money going out before you even know if the lead turns into a job.

The experience

On Google, a customer searches, sees real reviews, and calls the one business they picked — nobody else reaches out. On Angi or Thumbtack, a customer usually fills out one request and hears back from several competing businesses at once, because that same request gets shared with multiple pros. More options for the customer, but also more calls they didn't specifically ask for.

The competition

It's not just the shop down the street. Thumbtack, Angi, and HomeAdvisor already show up well for the exact same searches — a business's own site is competing with them too, everywhere a customer might look.

The science

For a Business Profile, Google says exactly what decides whether you show up: how close you are, how relevant you are to the search, and how established you look — reviews, activity, that kind of thing. For your actual website, Google has said directly that it rewards someone who's actually done the work — real experience — over a page that just describes it. That's Google's own stated policy, not a guess.

The economics

None of this is instant, and it isn't free of ongoing work either way. Even Sonshine Glass — the real client behind the $93K signed contract covered elsewhere on this site — doesn't have every single page showing up in Google search yet. Getting a full site fully visible takes ongoing work, not a switch you flip once. But the pages that are visible are the ones bringing in the calls.

How they actually compare

Not the same kind of thing, for either side of the transaction.

Google Angi Thumbtack
For the consumer Free — search, compare, and contact a business directly Free to browse and request quotes Free — submit a request, compare quotes
For the business No fee to appear or rank organically Pay to advertise and appear in search Pay per lead, whether or not it closes
Who else gets the lead No one — it's a direct call or form fill to you Typically sold to 3–5 pros at once Typically sold to multiple pros at once
What you're really buying Visibility you keep building on, indefinitely Visibility rented for as long as you keep paying A shot at a lead other pros already have too

The bigger picture

This isn't only a Google problem.

None of this works in isolation. A customer who can't reach anyone, or never hears back after asking for a quote, doesn't just go quiet — they sometimes leave a review that says exactly that. Google has said directly that reviews factor into whether a business looks established and trustworthy enough to show. A bad experience doesn't just cost the one job in front of you — it can quietly work against the visibility that's supposed to bring the next ten.
